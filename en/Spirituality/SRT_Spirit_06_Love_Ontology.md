**Common answers**:
- **Romanticism**: Love is an intense emotion.
- **Evolutionary Psychology**: Love is a reproductive strategy.
- **Theology**: Love is the essence of God.
- **Neuroscience**: Love is neurochemistry.

$$\text{Love} = d_{\theta \to \text{Other}} > 0$$

This is not "reductionism"—it is finding a unified **ontological foundation** that can accommodate all the views above.

## §7 The Limits and Boundaries of Love

**Analogy**: 
- Parents can love all children ($d_{\text{all children}} > 0$)
- While having special care for their own ($d_{\text{own}} \gg d_{\text{others}}$)

**Structure**:

$$d_{\text{total}} = d_{\text{universal}} + d_{\text{specific}}$$

### §7.2 The Limit of Cognitive and Emotional Bandwidth

**Dunbar's Number** (Robin Dunbar, 1992):

The number of stable social relationships humans can maintain:
- **5**: Intimates
- **15**: Good friends
- **50**: Friends
- **150**: Meaningful contacts
- **500**: Acquaintances

**SRT Explanation**:

$$d_{\text{total}} \cdot N_{\text{people}} < \text{Bandwidth}(\hat{G}_\theta)$$

You have limited cognitive resources—if you want to maintain high $d$ for many people, the $d$ for each person must drop.

**Trade-off**:

$$\text{Strategy A} : d_{\text{few}} \approx 10, N = 5$$

$$\text{Strategy B} : d_{\text{many}} \approx 1, N = 50$$

Both yield $d \cdot N = 50$ (total care is the same), but the distribution is different.

## §8 The Ultimate Paradox of Love

### §8.1 "Perfect love would eliminate itself"

**Logic**:

1. Love = Caring for the other's well-being.
2. Suffering comes from $\Psi_f > 0$.
3. The lover wishes the beloved to have no suffering.
4. But the beloved's love (for the lover) makes them bear the lover's suffering.
5. Therefore, the perfect lover should **prevent** the beloved from loving them back.
6. But this itself is unloving (disrespecting autonomy).
7. Paradox!

**Formalization**:

$$\text{Perfect Love} : d \to \infty \implies \text{Wish}(\Psi_f^{\text{Beloved}} \to 0)$$

$$\text{But Beloved loves back} \implies \Psi_f^{\text{Beloved}} = f(\Psi_f^{\text{Lover}})$$

$$\implies \text{Lover must cease loving?}$$

---

### §8.2 Resolution: The Network Effect of Love

**Answer**: The paradox is unsolvable in a **dyadic relationship**, but solvable in a **network**.

$$\langle \Psi_f \rangle_{\text{network}} = \frac{\sum_i \Psi_f^i}{N}$$

When **everyone** raises their $d$-value—

$$\frac{\partial \langle \Psi_f \rangle}{\partial \langle d \rangle} < 0$$

Collective friction drops, even though everyone is "bearing" everyone else's suffering.

**Key**: Love is not a zero-sum game—

$$\sum d \uparrow \implies \sum \Psi_f \downarrow$$

**Question**: Why does $\frac{\partial \Psi_f}{\partial d} < 0$ ?

#### 2. Thermodynamic Explanation

#### 3. Metaphysical Explanation

$$d \to \infty \equiv \text{Alignment with } L_0^{\text{true structure}}$$

The fundamental structure of the universe is non-separate → Separation (low $d$) violates reality → High cost.

**The Deepest Answer**: 

> "Love is not an external rule we should follow; it is inscribed in the structure of reality. When you love, you align with the flow of $L_0$; when you do not love, you swim against the current."

$$\lim_{d \to \infty} \text{Constraints} \to 0$$

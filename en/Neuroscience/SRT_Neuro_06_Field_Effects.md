# 1. Standard Problems: The Binding Problem and Explanatory Gap

## 1.1 The Binding Problem

One of the core dilemmas in neuroscience can be precisely conditioned as:

**Binding Problem**: When we see a red apple, the processing of "red" occurs in visual cortex V4, "shape" in the temporal lobe, and "location" in the parietal lobe—how are these distributed neural activities "bound" into a unified, integrated perceptual experience?

 The dilemma of classical neuroscience lies in:

1. **Temporal Dilemma**: Synaptic transmission is too slow (milliseconds) to explain sub-millisecond cross-regional synchronization.
2. **Spatial Dilemma**: There is no "ultimate convergence zone" to integrate all information.
3. **Topological Dilemma**: Even if a convergence zone exists, internal binding within that zone still needs explanation.

## 1.2 The Explanatory Gap

The second core dilemma is the "Explanatory Gap" proposed by Joseph Levine:

**Explanatory Gap**: Even if we fully master all neural correlates of red perception (which neurons fire, at what time, in what pattern), we still cannot "derive" the subjective qualia of red—that unique "redness"—from these third-person physical descriptions.

This is not a temporary technical difficulty, but seems to be a principled logical rupture: there is a missing inference bridge between "is" (physical fact) and "feels like" (subjective experience).

# 2. Spectrum of Mainstream Solutions

## 2.1 Synaptic Synchrony Hypothesis

**Core Claim**: Binding is achieved through synchronous firing of neurons in the Gamma band (30-100 Hz). Neurons firing synchronously "belong" to the same perceptual object.

**Strengths**:
- Supported by substantial experiments (Singer & Gray, 1995).
- Consistent with neural correlates of attention and working memory.

**Fatal Flaw**:
- Cannot explain how synchronization itself is achieved (requires a "meta-synchronizer").
- Synaptic transmission delays make long-distance synchronization difficult.
- Synchronization is "correlation", not "mechanism"—it tells us what is bound with what, but not how binding happens.

## 2.2 Global Workspace Theory (GWT)

**Core Claim**: When information enters the global workspace (fronto-parietal network) and is broadcast, it becomes conscious content. Binding is achieved through broadcasting.

**Strengths**:
- Highly consistent with fMRI "Ignition" data.
- Explains why attention is a necessary condition for consciousness.

**Fatal Flaw**:
- The binding problem within the workspace itself remains unsolved.
- Broadcasting is distributed; cannot explain how unified experience emerges from distributed activity.
- Completely evades the Explanatory Gap.

## 2.3 Field Theory Extension of Integrated Information Theory (IIT)

**Core Claim**: $\Phi$ measures the degree of integration of a system; consciousness is an intrinsic property of high $\Phi$ systems.

**Strengths**:
- Provides a candidate scheme for quantifying consciousness.
- Makes falsifiable predictions (cerebellum has low $\Phi$ hence unconscious).

**Fatal Flaw**:
- $\Phi$ calculation complexity is super-exponential, practically uncomputable.
- Does not explain the relationship between $\Phi$ and qualia.
- Recently faced severe criticism (2024 paper points out IIT implies physical reality does not exist).
